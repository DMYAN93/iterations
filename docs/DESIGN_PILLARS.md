# Iterations — Game Design Pillars

## Core Concept
A dynasty simulator with RPG, strategy, and simulation layers. The player takes the role of a researcher from a near-future civilization, interfacing with a simulation of a lost civilization in order to understand what happened to it. The simulation is the game. The researcher is the player's conscience.

Framing inspired by the Strugatsky brothers' *Hard to be God* — the tension between observation and intervention, between understanding history and trying to change it.

---

## The Researcher Frame
- The player is a researcher, not a god. They observe and can intervene, but intervention has cost.
- Saving and loading the game is an in-universe act — the researcher "restores a simulation checkpoint." This requires a rare resource generated by the dynasty's actions inside the simulation. Saving is a meaningful decision, not a safety net.
- The researcher's presence is felt but not prevalent. It surfaces in UI framing, in the cost of intervention, and in the meta-narrative of piecing together what destroyed this civilization.
- The win condition is not prosperity — it is understanding. Reconstructing the truth of what happened to this civilization is the researcher's goal. The player may choose to intervene and change that truth, but at a cost to their research integrity.
- Potential mechanic: a "research completeness" score that tracks how much of the civilization's true history has been uncovered, in tension with an "intervention score" that reflects how much the researcher has altered events.

## The Simulation

### Three-Faction Structure
- Three major factions form the baseline political conflict. Three-way conflicts are inherently unstable and dramatic — any two parties are tempted to ally against the third, but doing so strengthens their new ally.
- Each faction has internal sub-conflicts: hardliners vs. reformers, old guard vs. new blood, idealists vs. pragmatists. The player can engage at both levels — the grand faction conflict and the internal identity struggle.
- Factions can be shaped from within. A player who backs the right internal faction can change what that faction fundamentally is over generations. This is the deepest level of strategic play.
- Inspired by: New Vegas's faction structure, but with full commitment to internal faction complexity that New Vegas ran out of time to deliver.

### Characters and Agents
- Narratively focused — fewer meaningful characters rather than thousands of anonymous ones.
- Named characters drive the story. They have personalities, histories, relationships, and ambitions that exist independent of the player.
- Faction stats are derived bottom-up from their members — even "Peasant 12" contributes to faction strength, morale, and economy, but the narrative focuses on lords, heirs, and notable figures.
- Inspired by: Mount and Blade's named character layer over abstracted rank-and-file.
- Characters have memory. Past interactions shape future encounters — a lord you defeated who escaped, rebuilt, and returns with a personal grudge is not a scripted event, it emerges from the simulation.
- Inspired by: the spirit of the Nemesis system (Shadow of Mordor), implemented through simulation memory rather than a dedicated hierarchy mechanic.

### Time and Pacing
- Time flows naturally at the strategic layer — a calendar, aging, generational change. Inspired by CK3's time feeling.
- Two-speed gameplay: strategic layer (map, diplomacy, dynasty management, time flowing) and tactical layer (personal confrontations — battles, duels, negotiations — where strategic time freezes).
- The transition between layers is a zoom, not a mode switch. What happens in the tactical layer has direct consequences in the strategic layer.
- Characters age and die. Dynasties rise and fall. The researcher watches generations pass.

### The Regression Theme
- The civilization the researcher studies is in decline or has already fallen. The player uncovers why.
- Factions that destroy their intellectuals, suppress knowledge, or purge dissenters weaken in ways that take generations to manifest. A player who understands this can see disaster coming long before the simulation's inhabitants can.
- Directly inspired by *Hard to be God*'s central theme — the regression of knowledge and culture as a political and moral catastrophe.

## The RPG Layer
- Turn-based combat. The game is about decisions and consequences, not reflexes. Real-time combat would compete with the dynasty sim tone rather than serve it.
- Intimate scale — no abstract armies. Confrontations involve named characters with histories, not unit counts.
- Inspired by: Final Fantasy Tactics (political intrigue + tactical combat serving narrative), Fire Emblem (permadeath making individual characters matter), Battle Brothers (mercenary company with meaningful individual soldiers).
- Dialogue and character interaction is the heart of the RPG layer. Inspired by Fallout New Vegas — dense, morally complex, reactive to player history.
- Potential future feature: optional local LLM integration for dynamic dialogue. Characters defined by personality, history, and current emotional state generate contextually appropriate responses. Authored content provides the skeleton; generated content provides the flesh. Keeps quality high for key story moments while enabling reactivity at scale.

---

## The World
- 2D. Procedurally generated terrain with meaningful geography — rivers, mountain passes, fertile valleys — that affects faction behavior, military strategy, and economic development.
- The world exists before the player arrives and continues without them. Every faction has goals independent of the player.
- 3D is a potential future direction but not a current goal. Generated 3D worlds tend to feel significantly worse than generated 2D worlds.

## Tone and References
- *Hard to be God* — Strugatsky brothers. Core moral framing of the researcher narrative.
- *Roadside Picnic*, *The Inhabited Island* — Strugatsky brothers. Broader tonal reference.
- Crusader Kings 3 — dynasty simulation, emergent narrative, political systems.
- Victoria 3 — bottom-up economic simulation, agents driving aggregate stats.
- Mount and Blade — named characters over abstracted rank-and-file, personal stakes in a political world.
- Fallout New Vegas — dense reactive writing, faction depth, companion character quality.
- Final Fantasy Tactics — political narrative + tactical combat.
- Dwarf Fortress / RimWorld — emergent story from simulation systems, no win state.
- Kenshi — personal survival blending into faction-level strategy organically.

---

## What This Game Is
A story engine. The systems exist to generate meaningful moments between characters across generations. The player is simultaneously a dynasty leader making strategic decisions and a researcher watching history unfold, aware that their intervention has cost. The best sessions will produce stories that couldn't have been scripted — a dynasty that fell because of one bad marriage alliance three generations ago, a faction that reformed itself from within after a player-backed reformer finally won the internal struggle, a researcher who spent all their integrity resources trying to save one character and changed the course of the civilization's history in ways they didn't intend.

## Open Questions
- What destroyed the civilization the researcher is studying? Is this authored or emergent?
- How present is the researcher in the UI and narrative?
- What is the exact nature of the "simulation integrity" resource — how is it earned, how is it spent?
- Does the tactical layer use a grid (FFT-style) or is it more abstract?
- How much of the narrative is authored vs. procedurally generated?
- At what point does a "Peasant 12" become a named character? Is it triggered by player interaction, by faction events, or randomly?
